The Challenge of Artificial Humor

Requiring a good dose of self-awareness, expressivity, communication skills and timing, a good sense of humor is definitely important to make intelligent machines more human.

By Gabriele Sarti | October 11, 2017 
"Humor generation is a very hard problem. It is difficult to say exactly what makes a joke funny, and solving this problem algorithmically is assumed to require deep semantic understanding, as well as cultural and other contextual cues."

Even if four years have passed since Saša Petrović and David Matthews published their paper “Unsupervised joke generation from big data”, their abstract is still actual in 2017. In fact, the two researchers from the University of Edinburgh created a simple model that could generate jokes by inserting specific X, Y, Z related words in the sentence “I like my X like I like my Y, Z”.
Even if a large amount of data scraped from Twitter was used to train the model, the percentage of funniness of the output was estimated around 33%, less than half of the result of a common human joke. Similar result were obtained by many other researchers that specialize in computational humor, a rather new branch of linguistics which applies the principles of artificial intelligence in order to understand the mechanisms behind human humor.

More recently, many efforts were put into creating a basic sense of humor that could be implemented in chatbots and virtual assistants. Since the smartphone market skyrocketed in the last few years, those technologies become widespread among the population, making natural and unformal conversation almost a necessity. As Mark Riedl, a computational humor expert from Georgia Institute of Technology puts it, “Humor can be used to put people at ease and create rapport. This will be crucial where we’re building societies surrounded by AIs.”

 "Humor can be used to put people at ease and create rapport. This will be crucial where we're building societies surrounded by AIs." - Mark Riedl
But we are still light years away from the examples of funny robots movies gave us, such as the robot TARS from Nolan’s masterpiece Interstellar, or Bender and the others witty robots from the TV show Futurama. The main problem behind all this is the context and self-awareness necessary to make an effective joke. Indeed, it is quite common to hear the sentence “I don’t even know why I find this funny!”, which describes perfectly the challenges that surround a direct translation of human humor into a computational model.
​
More of it, the sense of humor is totally subjective, and may vary depending on many factors. Even with all those uncertainties, artificial intelligences are slowly becoming better at understanding what is funny and thus creating their own personal humor. More than often, though, we find the comedic framing to be funnier than the jokes themselves. A good example is provided by Data, one of the first examples of robotic stand-up comedy, which attitude and stance are more hilarious than the jokes themselves.
​Despite all the difficulties researchers have encountered, future looks promising for this branch of artificial intelligence. Simple models are being deconstructed in order to create standards for funniness. An example of that is given by Google Magenta, a Google open source research project which has recently focused on creating a punch-line bot. This AI would generate structures called truth vectors, which create a semantic context behind a sentence which could be manipulated in order to introduce a punch factor. 
Computational humor is still a very young field, and we’ll probably see more breakthrough discoveries in the near future. The revolution brought by artificial humor definitely won’t be a joke.
